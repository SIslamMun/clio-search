\section{Conclusion}

Scientific data discovery requires search that reasons, not just retrieves. Current retrieval systems cannot inspect a corpus before querying it, cannot adapt their strategy to metadata quality, and cannot perform the arithmetic needed to match measurements across unit conventions. We addressed this gap with CLIO Search, which separates search reasoning from search execution through an observe-decide-act-evaluate cycle implemented as a four-stage pipeline.

The results validate this approach across multiple evaluation scales. On cross-unit queries, CLIO achieves 0.99 P@5 where the strongest text baseline scores 0.40, with correctness guaranteed by construction for all 46 supported unit transformations. On same-unit controls, all methods perform comparably (0.30--0.45), confirming the gains are specific to the domain-reasoning gap that CLIO addresses. Real-world validation on 288 NOAA observatory documents shows 87\% precision improvement. At distributed scale on 2.5M arXiv abstracts across 4 GH200 nodes on NCSA DeltaAI, cross-unit queries return identical results at sub-50\,ms latency with 0.94 weak scaling efficiency. IOWarp integration demonstrates that CLIO can serve as the search layer for tiered blob storage, inspecting 0.5\% of the corpus where the native API returns everything unfiltered. A deterministic fallback achieves 96\% of LLM-assisted quality, confirming that the science-aware operators, not the language model, are the primary source of gains.

The unit registry covers 58 units across 12 SI domains; extending coverage requires one configuration entry per unit. Compound units and free-text unit parsing remain unsupported. Scaling to petabyte-scale parallel filesystems with billions of blobs remains future work. The principle that scientific data discovery requires search systems that reason about how to search, not just execute queries, extends to any domain where data is heterogeneous, metadata is inconsistent, and correctness is non-negotiable.
