\section{Conclusion}

Scientific data discovery requires search that reasons, not just retrieves. Three gaps make automation hard: \emph{dimensional blindness} (retrieval systems cannot perform the arithmetic needed to match measurements across unit conventions), \emph{metadata heterogeneity} (systems cannot inspect a corpus before querying or adapt their strategy to what metadata exists), and \emph{storage fragmentation} (no engine federates across the heterogeneous HPC backends where scientific data lives). We addressed all three with CLIO Search, which separates search reasoning from search execution through an observe-decide-act-evaluate cycle implemented as a four-stage pipeline.

The results validate this approach. On cross-unit queries, CLIO achieves 0.99~P@5 where the strongest text baseline scores 0.40, with correctness guaranteed by construction for all supported unit transformations. Real-world validation on 288~NOAA observatory documents shows 87\% precision improvement. At distributed scale on 2.5M~arXiv abstracts across 4~GH200 nodes on NCSA DeltaAI, cross-unit queries return in under 50\,ms. Together, these results confirm that science-aware arithmetic operators close a gap that neither learned embeddings nor text normalization can address.

The unit registry covers 58 units across 12 SI domains; extending coverage requires one configuration entry per unit. Compound units and free-text unit parsing remain unsupported. Scaling to petabyte-scale parallel filesystems with billions of blobs remains future work. The principle that scientific data discovery requires search systems that reason about how to search, not just execute queries, extends to any domain where data is heterogeneous, metadata is inconsistent, and correctness is non-negotiable.
